Capella 67

Still becoming. Always open.

Walking through Tel Aviv, listening to a podcast about a house in Los Angeles, I felt I was hearing the story of a relative.

The house could not have been more different from ours in Ramello. Different continent, different architecture, different community. Yet I recognized something immediately.

Not the building itself.

The social role it played.

The podcast told the story of a house that had accumulated people, projects, memories, and community over decades. As I listened, I found myself thinking about another house, thousands of kilometers away, perched above a path in a small mountain village in Northern Italy.

A much older house. A much quieter house.

And somehow, kin.

The house in Ramello is probably a few hundred years old. Nobody seems to know exactly. Long enough that certainty has given way to stories.

The ground floor once housed animals. That was its purpose. The architecture made sense in a world where humans and animals shared the same structure and depended on one another.

That world disappeared.

The animals left.

The village changed.

The house remained.

Today, that former stable is Capella 67, a small refuge open to whoever happens to pass by.

You push open the door and enter.

Inside there is a small library. Many of the books are about nature, mountains, travel, wandering. Thoreau's Walden sits there, almost obligatorily. Other books arrive in less predictable ways. Somebody leaves one behind. Months later another appears. I rarely know where they come from.

There is a guest book.

People often write the same thing in different languages.

Thank you.

What a surprise.

I can't believe this place exists.

I understand the reaction. I sometimes feel the same way.

There are candles, a few simple musical instruments, a wood stove, and a pile of firewood. In winter the pile gradually shrinks. I never know exactly who used it. Somebody must have come in from the cold, lit a fire, sat for a while, and moved on.

I like that I don't know. Most days, the Capella contains traces of people more than people themselves.

What interests me is that spaces like this once existed everywhere.

We tend to think of homes as private spaces and public life as something that happens elsewhere. But that division is relatively recent. For much of history, houses were more porous. Travelers arrived. Neighbors wandered in. Work happened at home. Commerce happened at home. Hospitality happened at home.

Many cultures developed spaces that sat between public and private. The Japanese engawa (縁側). The British public house. Porches, courtyards, workshops, porticos.

Threshold spaces.

Places where one could arrive without quite being a guest and stay without quite belonging.

The Capella feels related to these places.

Not because it copies them.

Because it asks a similar question.

What happens when a space is available?

Not reserved. Not programmed. Just available.

The sociologist Ray Oldenburg argued that healthy societies depend on "third places": spaces distinct from home and work where informal social life can flourish. Cafés, pubs, barber shops, and public squares have traditionally fulfilled this role.

Capella 67 differs from these examples in one important respect.

It is neither commercial nor institutional.

No transaction takes place there. Nobody is employed to run it. Nobody is required to attend. It survives through trust, hospitality, and the willingness of strangers to participate in something they did not create.

Sometimes on Friday mornings a handful of people gather there to write, think, brainstorm, or drink coffee while pretending to write. Around lunchtime we might walk down to the river and jump in.

Sometimes musicians come through.

Last summer, nearly sixty people showed up for a concert. Italians, Israelis, neighbors, friends, curious passersby. People spread across the terrace, the path, the house, the Capella. Nobody seemed particularly concerned about where one space ended and another began.

For a few hours, the entire place felt like a single room.

The next morning there was laundry to do.

The dishes still needed washing.

The house became a house again.

And yet something remained.

Another memory attached itself to the building.

Another layer.

That is the other thing that fascinates me about old houses.

They are never finished.

The people who built this house could not have imagined free-writing mornings, community concerts, or visitors from half a dozen countries sitting in a former stable discussing their lives. Yet somehow they participated in those moments. They laid stones that would support futures they could not foresee.

The Capella is one of those futures.

The upper floor may become another.

At the moment, its purpose remains undecided.

I like that.

There is pressure these days to optimize everything, assign every room a function, define every project. But sometimes a place should be allowed to wait.

The unfinished upper floor is waiting.

Not empty.

Waiting.

Maybe somebody will discover what it wants to become.

Near the stove there is a pile of river stones.

At some point I left them there without much thought.

Then a visitor stacked a few into a small cairn.

Later another appeared.

Then another.

Nobody asked permission. Nobody wrote instructions.

People simply understood.

The cairns keep growing.

I have come to think that they capture something essential about the Capella.

Each one is small.

Each one depends on somebody having come before.

Each one invites the next person to add something.

Not much.

Just a stone.

The house itself feels a bit like that.

A few hundred years ago somebody built it.

Later somebody cared for it.

Later somebody abandoned part of it.

Later somebody opened a room.

Visitors left books.

Visitors wrote in the guest book.

Visitors burned wood.

Visitors stacked stones.

None of these contributions are large. Most are barely noticeable.

But together they have created something.

I sometimes wonder whether the Capella exists because of a need in the village or because of a bias in my own thinking.

I like open systems.

I like unfinished projects.

I like discovering what people do when they are trusted.

Perhaps the Capella is simply a physical manifestation of those preferences.

But then I read the guest book, see another cairn added to the pile, or find a book that arrived from somewhere unknown, and I begin to suspect that the impulse runs deeper than personal taste.

Most houses contain an unused room.

Most villages contain an unused building.

What would happen if one of them were opened?

Not permanently.

Not heroically.

Just enough for something unexpected to emerge.

Maybe the question is not whether every village needs a Capella.

Maybe the question is what your village, your street, your building, or your home might become if one door were left open.
